COVID-19 Vaccination and Disease Severity in Germany

Introduction

This project investigates the development of COVID-19 vaccination coverage in Germany and explores whether vaccination rates were associated with measures of disease severity during the pandemic.

We use publicly available data from the Our World in Data COVID-19 dataset, restricted to Germany.

Dataset

The dataset contains daily observations for Germany between 2020 and 2024.

For our analyses we mainly use the following variables:

  • date
  • people_vaccinated_per_hundred
  • new_deaths_smoothed
  • icu_patients
  • new_cases_smoothed (for context)

Before the analysis, we inspected the dataset for missing values, variable types, and implausible observations.

Research Questions

Our project addresses the following questions:

  1. How did COVID-19 vaccination coverage in Germany change over time?

  2. Was higher vaccination coverage associated with lower COVID-19 severity?

    • Was higher vaccination coverage associated with lower COVID-19 mortality?
    • Was higher vaccination coverage associated with lower ICU occupancy?

Vaccination and COVID-19 Severity - Research Question 2

Model specification — RQ2a

  • Response: new_deaths_smoothed — daily new confirmed COVID-19 deaths, 7-day smoothed (<dbl>)

  • Predictor: people_vaccinated_per_hundred — share of people with at least one dose (<dbl>)

  • Filter: restricted to rows where both variables are not missing (vaccination reporting only begins in late December 2020, which is the binding constraint)

  • Method: Pearson correlation between the two variables, tested with the theory-based {infer} pipeline (specify() → assume("t") / calculate(stat = "correlation")), plus a scatter plot to inspect the shape of the relationship

  • Expected output: the observed correlation coefficient, a p-value, a 95% confidence interval, and a scatter plot of mortality against vaccination coverage

  • Interpretation: the sign of the coefficient tells us whether days with higher vaccination coverage tended to have lower mortality; because both variables move over time, the association is exploratory and no causal claims are made

Model specification — RQ2b

  • Response: icu_patients — number of COVID-19 patients in intensive care on a given day (<dbl>)

  • Predictor: people_vaccinated_per_hundred — share of people with at least one dose (<dbl>)

  • Filter: restricted to rows where both variables are not missing (same binding constraint as RQ2a)

  • Method: same as RQ2a — Pearson correlation via the theory-based {infer} pipeline, plus a scatter plot

  • Expected output: the observed correlation coefficient, a p-value, a 95% confidence interval, and a scatter plot of ICU occupancy against vaccination coverage

  • Interpretation: the sign of the coefficient tells us whether days with higher vaccination coverage tended to have lower ICU occupancy; the ICU lower-bound artefact (minimum value of exactly 100, see quality checks) is kept in mind when interpreting low values, and no causal claims are made

Can the data support our RQ2? – Sanity Check

As for RQ1, we first confirm that the variables exist with the correct types using glimpse() (see the RQ1 sanity check above): people_vaccinated_per_hundred, new_deaths_smoothed and icu_patients are all doubles, and date is a Date, which is what the planned correlation analysis and scatter plots require.

For RQ2a we observe a moderate negative correlation of about r = -0.53 (95% CI roughly -0.58 to -0.48), and for RQ2b a somewhat stronger negative correlation of about r = -0.65 (95% CI roughly -0.69 to -0.61). One-sentence interpretation (Ex 2.4): In the period where vaccination data exist, days with higher vaccination coverage tended to have lower COVID-19 mortality and lower ICU occupancy — an exploratory, associational finding that is consistent with our research questions but does not establish a causal effect of vaccination.
